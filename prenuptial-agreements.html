Prenuptial Agreements

Protecting your future with thoughtful planning before marriage.

Why Consider a Prenuptial Agreement?

A prenuptial agreement isn't about expecting your marriage to fail—it's about having honest conversations about finances, protecting both partners, and planning for the future. Many couples find that creating a prenup actually strengthens their relationship by encouraging open communication about important topics.

Prenuptial agreements can protect premarital assets, clarify financial responsibilities during marriage, protect family businesses or inheritances, and provide certainty about what would happen in the event of divorce. They're especially valuable when one or both partners have significant assets, own a business, have children from previous relationships, or expect to receive inheritances.

At Kovarsky Law, we help couples create fair, enforceable prenuptial agreements that protect both parties' interests while respecting the partnership you're building together.

What a Prenup Can Address

Property & Asset Protection

Define what property remains separate, how marital property will be divided, protect family heirlooms or inheritances, and establish how property acquired during marriage will be characterized.

Business Interests

Protect ownership in a business you started before marriage, establish how business growth during marriage will be handled, and ensure business operations aren't disrupted by divorce proceedings.

Debt Responsibility

Specify who is responsible for debts brought into the marriage and how debts incurred during marriage will be allocated. Protect yourself from a spouse's pre-existing debts.

Spousal Support

Establish whether spousal maintenance (alimony) will be paid in the event of divorce, and if so, the amount and duration. This provides clarity and predictability for both parties.

Creating an Enforceable Agreement

For a prenuptial agreement to be enforceable in Colorado, certain requirements must be met:

Written & Signed

Prenuptial agreements must be in writing and signed by both parties. Oral agreements are not enforceable.

Full Disclosure

Both parties must fully disclose their assets, debts, and income. Hidden assets can invalidate the agreement.

Voluntary Agreement

Both parties must sign voluntarily, without coercion or duress. Adequate time to review the agreement is important.

Independent Legal Counsel

Each party should have their own attorney review the agreement. This helps ensure fairness and understanding of the terms.

Common Questions

When should we start the prenup process?

Ideally, begin discussions several months before your wedding. This allows time for honest conversations, proper drafting, legal review by both parties' attorneys, and avoids any appearance of last-minute pressure.

Can a prenup address child custody or support?

No. Colorado courts will not enforce prenuptial provisions regarding child custody or child support. These matters must be determined based on the children's best interests at the time of divorce.

What if we're already married?

Couples who are already married can enter into a postnuptial agreement, which addresses similar issues but is created after marriage. The requirements for enforceability are similar to prenuptial agreements.

Can a prenup be modified later?

Yes, prenuptial agreements can be modified after marriage if both parties agree. Any modifications should be in writing and signed by both parties to be enforceable.
