Divorce Questions

Colorado has a mandatory 91-day waiting period from the date of filing before a divorce can be finalized. Uncontested divorces where both parties agree on all terms can often be completed shortly after this waiting period ends.

Contested divorces involving disputes over property, custody, or support can take 6-12 months or longer depending on complexity. I'll give you a realistic timeline based on the specific circumstances of your case.

Legal separation addresses all the same issues as divorce—property division, custody, support—but you remain legally married. Some couples choose legal separation for religious reasons, to maintain health insurance benefits, or because they're not yet certain about divorce.

A legal separation can later be converted to a divorce if circumstances change. The process and timeline are similar to divorce.

Colorado is an "equitable distribution" state, meaning marital property is divided fairly—but not necessarily equally. The court considers factors like each spouse's economic circumstances, contributions to the marriage, and the value of property each spouse is receiving.

Separate property (assets owned before marriage or received as gifts/inheritance) typically remains with the original owner. Complex assets like businesses, retirement accounts, and real estate require careful valuation and division.

No. Colorado is a "no-fault" divorce state. You only need to state that the marriage is "irretrievably broken." You don't need to prove adultery, abuse, or any other wrongdoing. However, certain behaviors may still be relevant when determining custody or dividing assets.

Child Custody Questions

Colorado uses the terms "parenting time" and "decision-making responsibility" instead of "custody." Parenting time refers to the schedule of when children are with each parent. Decision-making responsibility covers who makes major decisions about the children's education, healthcare, religion, and extracurricular activities.

These can be shared equally, divided by category, or allocated primarily to one parent depending on what's in the children's best interests.

Colorado courts make custody decisions based on the "best interests of the child." Factors include each parent's relationship with the child, the child's adjustment to home and school, each parent's ability to encourage a relationship with the other parent, and any history of domestic violence or substance abuse.

The court generally favors arrangements that allow children to have meaningful relationships with both parents, unless safety concerns exist.

Yes, custody orders can be modified if there's been a substantial change in circumstances since the original order was entered. Common reasons include relocation, changes in work schedules, changes in the child's needs, or safety concerns.

Within the first two years, modifications are more difficult to obtain unless the child's safety is at risk. After two years, the standard becomes more flexible.

Colorado law requires a parent to provide written notice at least 90 days before relocating with children. If you object to the move, you can request a hearing where the relocating parent must prove the move is in the children's best interests.

These cases are fact-specific and often contentious. If you're facing a relocation situation—whether you want to move or object to a move—early legal guidance is crucial.

Child Support Questions

Colorado uses a formula based on both parents' gross incomes, the number of children, and the parenting time schedule. The state provides child support guidelines and worksheets that calculate the presumptive support amount.

Adjustments may be made for healthcare costs, childcare expenses, and other factors. In some cases, the court can deviate from the guidelines if the result would be unjust.

Yes. Either parent can request a modification if there's been a substantial and continuing change in circumstances—typically a significant change in income or parenting time. Generally, the support amount must change by at least 10% under the new calculation to warrant modification.

If you've lost your job or experienced a major income change, it's important to file for modification promptly. Support obligations continue to accrue at the original rate until a new order is entered.

Colorado takes child support enforcement seriously. Options include wage garnishment, intercepting tax refunds, suspending driver's licenses or professional licenses, and in severe cases, contempt of court proceedings that can result in jail time.

If you're owed child support, I can help you pursue enforcement through the court system or coordinate with the Colorado Child Support Services program.
