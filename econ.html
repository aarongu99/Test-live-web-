SkillDefinition

Five Steps to Think Critically

StepWhat It Involves
1. Adopt the attitudeOpen-Mindedness, Healthy Skepticism, Intellectual Humility, Free Thinking, High Motivation
2. Recognize hindrancesCognitive efficiency, social challenges, emotions, cognitive dissonance, information overload
3. Identify argumentsFind claim, evidence, warrant, qualifications
4. Evaluate argumentsCheck assumptions, evidence relevance, sufficiency, omissions
5. Evaluate sourcesCheck qualifications, reputation, motive for bias, honesty/integrity

Structure of an Argument

Argument = Evidence + Warrant + Claim + [Sometimes] Qualifications

ComponentDefinitionExample
ClaimThe point being made (conclusion, proposition)"Don't trust John"
EvidenceGrounds for the claim (reasons, data, proofs)"he's a politician"
WarrantBridge between claim & evidence"because"
QualificationsConcessions/modifiers (when, if)Not always present

Internet History Timeline

1962

J.C.R. Licklider â€” "Galactic Network"

Licklider proposed the earliest ideas of a global computer network at BBN in a series of memos discussing the "Intergalactic Computer Network" concept. These ideas contained almost everything that the Internet is today, including cloud computing.

1969

ARPANET Established

The Advanced Research Projects Agency Network (ARPANET) was an early packet switching network and the first to implement the protocol suite TCP/IP. Both technologies became the technical foundation of the Internet. UCLA's Leonard Kleinrock's team sent the inaugural message "login".

1974

TCP Was Born

Vinton Cerf and Robert Elliot Kahn designed TCP during 1973 and published it in 1974. They are widely credited with inventing the Internet as we know it.

1984

DNS Was Born

Paul Mockapetris and Jon Postel introduced the Domain Name System with familiar suffixes: .com, .org, .edu, .net, .gov.

1985

First Domain Name Registered

Symbolics.com â€” registered by a Massachusetts computer company, making it the first domain name ever registered.

1986

Cisco Launches First Router

Cisco launched its first routing innovation, the AGS multi-protocol router.

1990

HTML Introduced

Tim Berners-Lee of CERN introduced HTML.

1991

World's First Website

Tim Berners-Lee introduced the World Wide Web (WWW) and launched the world's first website.

Packet Switching vs. Circuit Switching

FeatureCircuit SwitchingPacket Switching

EfficiencyDedicated line â€” less efficientMost efficient route for each packet
ReassemblyNot needed â€” continuous streamPackets reassembled at destination

Packet switching is a critical enabler of the Internet infrastructure.

Paul Baran and Donald Davies independently developed packet switching, a key element in ARPANET's success.

TCP/IP

The suite of protocols (communications standards) that enable data transmission via packet switching.

ProtocolRole
TCP (Transmission Control Protocol)Breaks data into packets, reassembles at receiving end, acknowledges receipt. Provides reliable, ordered, and error-checked delivery.
IP (Internet Protocol)Routes packets from one location to the next until they reach their destination.

Open-Architecture Networking (Kahn, 1972)

Four key principles:

  • No changes required of member networks
  • Communication on a best effort basis
  • "Black boxes" (gateways/routers) would connect the networks
  • No global control at the operations level

IP Addresses

IP Address: A unique numeric identifier for an Internet host (device on the Internet). Every host must have an IP address.

VersionFormatCapacityExample
IPv4Four segments (octets)232 addresses129.21.1.40
IPv6Hexadecimal, eight segments2128 addresses2001:0DB8:AC10:FE01:...

The Domain Name System (DNS) converts IP addresses into more easily remembered uniform resource locators (URLs).

Information Systems vs. Management Information Systems

Information Systems (IS) â€” An integrated set of components for collecting, storing, and processing data and for providing information, knowledge, and digital products.

Management Information Systems (MIS) â€” IS that are used to support managerial decision-making.

MIS as a discipline: The practice of designing, managing, and aligning the people, business processes, and information technology of an organization to accomplish a business objective.

The MIS Triangle

The three interconnected components of every management information system:

ComponentDefinitionFedEx COSMOS Example
PeopleThe actors who interact in a business processCouriers, Customer Service Reps, IT Staff, Managers
Business ProcessesGoal-oriented activities conducted by a businessReal-time update & tracking (replaced manual updates)
Information TechnologyHardware, software, and database used to facilitate business activitiesHand-held devices, advanced package tracking system, enhanced network infrastructure

Information Technology (IT)

IT = the combination of computer technology (hardware and software) and telecommunications technology (data, image, and voice networks) used in the processing and distribution of data.

Hardware & Software

Hardware â€” The physical components that enable computing: CPU, storage, input devices, output devices, communications devices.

Software â€” The sets of instructions (computer programs) that enable computers to perform tasks, plus the encoded data programs use.

TypePurposeExamples
System SoftwareOrganizes and controls hardware; coordinates other softwareOperating systems (Windows, macOS, Linux)
Application SoftwareEnables users to perform specific tasksWord processors, spreadsheets, web browsers

The IPO+S Model

The four core functions of information technology:

FunctionWhat It Does
InputReceiving data from outside sources
ProcessingManipulating data to create information
OutputProviding the results of the processing
StorageStoring data for later use

Feedback loops connect output back to input for system control and improvement.

Business Processes

A standardized set of activities that accomplish a specific task. They transform a set of inputs into a set of outputs (goods/services) for another person or process by using people and tools.

"How we do what we do"

Examples: Order Entry, Order Fulfillment, R&D, Manufacturing, Marketing, Financial Reporting, Strategic Planning, Inventory Management, Product Distribution, Materials Sourcing.

People & Stakeholders in IS

Stakeholders = anyone who has an interest in an existing or proposed information system. Human beings are the most complex part of any organizational IS â€” and the most difficult to change.

StakeholderRoleExamples

The Systems Analyst

"A specialist who studies the problems and needs of an organization to determine how people, data, processes, and information technology can best be combined to bring about improvements."

A systems analyst is like a Bridge (connecting business and tech) and an Interpreter (translating between stakeholders).

Purpose & Impact of Information Systems

"Every information system is designed to make someone's life easier."

IS provides information needed to do a job, simplifies work, supports decision-making, improves understanding of customers, and enables communication. When we design IS, we are designing people's worlds â€” the design and implementation can make or break a business.

Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) integrates all departments and functions throughout an organization onto a single IT system so employees can make decisions using enterprise-wide information.

Benefits: Integrated systems, consistent interface, one database, access to current data, improved planning capabilities, multinational capabilities.

CRM is about creating and maintaining relationships with customers â€” a holistic process of identifying, attracting, differentiating, and retaining customers. Focuses on large customer databases with operational and analytical functionality.

Collections vs. Systems

A system is "something that maintains its existence and functions through the interaction of its parts." A collection is merely a group of items without meaningful interaction.

SystemsCollections
Football team, MarriageKitchen items, Toolbox, Bowl of fruit, Database of customer names

Test: Do the parts interact to maintain the whole? If yes â†’ System. If they're just grouped together â†’ Collection.

Four key properties define how systems behave:

PropertyMeaning
Systems have purposesEvery system exists to accomplish something
All parts must be presentFor the system to carry out its purpose optimally
Order mattersThe arrangement of parts affects system performance
