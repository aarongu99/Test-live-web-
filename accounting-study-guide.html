Chapter 4: Reporting & Analyzing Merchandising Operations

Merchandiser vs. Service Company

Key Concept

Service companies sell time/services (e.g., law firms, accountants). Income = Revenue − Expenses.

Merchandisers buy products and resell them (e.g., Walmart, Home Depot). They have an additional line: Cost of Goods Sold (COGS).

Net Sales − Cost of Goods Sold = Gross Profit
Gross Profit − Operating Expenses = Net Income

The balance sheet for a merchandiser is the same as a service company except for one additional current asset: Merchandise Inventory.

Operating Cycle for a Merchandiser

Key Concept

Cash → Purchase Inventory → Credit Sales → Accounts Receivable → Collect Cash

Companies try to keep their operating cycles short because assets tied up in inventory and receivables are not productive.

Perpetual vs. Periodic Inventory Systems

Key Concept

Perpetual: Continuously updates Merchandise Inventory for each purchase and sale. COGS is recorded at time of sale. Most common with modern technology.

Periodic: Only updates inventory records at end of period. Uses temporary accounts (Purchases, Purchase Returns, etc.). COGS is calculated at period end.

Beginning Inventory + Net Purchases = Goods Available for Sale
Goods Available − Ending Inventory = Cost of Goods Sold

Credit Terms & Purchase Discounts

Journal Entry

Credit terms specify payment timing. Example: 2/10, n/30 means 2% discount if paid within 10 days; full amount due in 30 days.

Dr. Accounts Payable .................. 500

Purchase Returns & Allowances

Journal Entry

Purchase return: Buyer sends merchandise back to seller.

Purchase allowance: Price reduction for defective/unacceptable goods kept by buyer.

Buyer issues a debit memorandum to notify seller.

Dr. Accounts Payable .................. XX
Cr. Merchandise Inventory ......... XX

Important: After a return, discounts apply only to the remaining balance of the invoice.

Exam Favorite

Multiple-Step vs. Single-Step Income Statement

Key Concept

Multiple-step has three main sections:

1. Gross Profit = Net Sales − COGS

2. Income from Operations = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses (Selling + G&A)

3. Net Income = Income from Operations ± Nonoperating items

Operating expenses are split into: Selling Expenses (promoting/delivering sales) and General & Administrative Expenses (office, HR, finance).

Single-step: Groups all revenues together, all expenses together (including COGS), one subtraction → Net Income.

Acid-Test Ratio & Gross Margin Ratio

Formula
Acid-Test Ratio = Quick Assets / Current Liabilities

Quick Assets = Cash + Short-Term Investments + Current Receivables
(Excludes inventory and prepaid expenses!)

Rule of thumb: ≥ 1.0 is adequate
Gross Margin Ratio = Gross Margin / Net Sales

Gross Margin = Net Sales − COGS

Measures profitability before operating expenses

Closing Entries for Merchandiser (Perpetual)

Journal Entry

Step 1: Close credit balance temp accounts (Sales) to Income Summary

Step 2: Close debit balance temp accounts (Sales Discounts, Sales R&A, COGS, all expenses) to Income Summary

Step 3: Close Income Summary to Retained Earnings

Step 4: Close Dividends to Retained Earnings

Chapter 5: Reporting & Analyzing Inventories

Determining Inventory Items

Key Concept

Merchandise inventory includes all goods a company owns and holds for sale, regardless of location.

Goods in Transit: Ownership depends on FOB terms. FOB shipping point = buyer's inventory when shipped. FOB destination = buyer's inventory after arrival.

Goods on Consignment: Consignor owns the goods; consignee sells them. Inventory stays on consignor's books — never on consignee's.

Damaged/Obsolete Goods: Not in inventory if unsalable. If salable, reported at net realizable value (sales price − selling costs).

Inventory Costing Methods Overview

Key Concept

Four methods to assign costs:

MethodAssumptionAdvantage
Specific IDEach item tracked individuallyExactly matches costs & revenues
FIFOFirst items purchased are first soldEnding inventory ≈ current replacement cost
LIFOLast items purchased are first soldBetter matches current costs to revenues
Weighted AvgAverage cost of all available unitsSmooths out price changes

Important: Physical flow does NOT need to match cost flow assumption!

FIFO vs. LIFO in Rising Prices

Exam Favorite

When prices are rising:

MeasureFIFOLIFO
Ending InventoryHighest (newest costs)Lowest (oldest costs)
COGSLowest (oldest costs)Highest (newest costs)
Gross ProfitHighestLowest
Net IncomeHighestLowest
TaxesHighestLowest

LIFO Conformity Rule: IRS requires that if LIFO is used for tax purposes, it must also be used for financial reporting.

When prices are declining, the effects reverse.

Weighted Average Calculations

Formula

Periodic system:

Weighted Avg Cost/Unit = Total Cost of Goods Available / Total Units Available

COGS = Units Sold × Weighted Avg Cost/Unit
Ending Inv = Units Remaining × Weighted Avg Cost/Unit

Perpetual system: Average is recalculated after each purchase, not just once at period end. This means perpetual weighted average often differs from periodic weighted average.

Lower of Cost or Market (LCM)

Key Concept

Inventory must be reported at the lower of cost or market (replacement cost). Supports the conservatism principle.

Can be applied three ways: (1) to each individual item, (2) to major categories, or (3) to entire inventory.

Example: Item A: Cost $170,000, Market $140,000 → Use $140,000
Item B: Cost $50,000, Market $60,000 → Use $50,000 (cost is lower)
Total LCM = $190,000 (not $220,000 cost)
Dr. Cost of Goods Sold ............... 30,000
Cr. Merchandise Inventory ......... 30,000

Effects of Inventory Errors

Exam Favorite

Ending Inventory UNDERSTATED:

→ COGS overstated → Net Income understated → Assets & Equity understated

Ending Inventory OVERSTATED:

→ COGS understated → Net Income overstated → Assets & Equity overstated

Self-correcting: Errors carry into the next period with opposite effects (since ending inventory becomes next period's beginning inventory). Over two years, total net income is correct.

Memory trick: Ending Inventory and Net Income move in the same direction. COGS and Net Income move in opposite directions.

Inventory Turnover & Days' Sales in Inventory

Formula
Inventory Turnover = COGS / Average Inventory

Average Inventory = (Beginning Inv + Ending Inv) / 2

Higher is better — means inventory sells quickly
Days' Sales in Inventory = (Ending Inventory / COGS) × 365

Lower is better — fewer days to sell inventory

Consistency & Full Disclosure Principles

Key Concept

Consistency principle: Use the same inventory method period after period for comparability.

Full-disclosure principle: If changing methods, must disclose the type of change, justification, and effect on income in the notes.

Different methods can be used for different inventory categories, as long as each category is consistent.

Retail Inventory & Gross Profit Methods (Appendix)

Formula

Retail Inventory Method (estimates ending inventory):

Step 1: Goods Available at Retail − Net Sales = Ending Inv at Retail
Step 2: Cost-to-Retail Ratio = Goods Available at Cost / Goods Available at Retail
Step 3: Ending Inv at Cost = Ending Inv at Retail × Cost-to-Retail Ratio

Gross Profit Method:

Estimated COGS = Net Sales × (1 − Gross Profit Rate)
Estimated Ending Inv = Goods Available at Cost − Estimated COGS

Chapter 6: Reporting & Analyzing Cash and Internal Controls

Internal Control System — Purpose & Overview

Key Concept

An internal control system consists of all policies and procedures managers use to:

1. Protect assets from waste, fraud, and inefficiency
2. Ensure reliable accounting (accurate financial reports)
3. Promote efficient operations
4. Urge adherence to company policies

Why it matters: Internal controls do NOT provide absolute guarantees — they reduce the risk of loss and errors. Every company, regardless of size, needs some form of internal controls to protect against fraud and mismanagement.

Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX): Passed in 2002 in response to accounting scandals (Enron, WorldCom). It requires:

• Managers of public companies must document and assess all internal controls impacting financial reporting (Section 404)
• External auditors must evaluate internal controls
• Penalties up to 25 years in prison with fines for violators
• PCAOB (Public Company Accounting Oversight Board) oversees auditor work

COSO Framework — 5 Components

Key Concept

The Committee of Sponsoring Organizations (COSO) identifies five interrelated ingredients of internal control:

ComponentWhat It Means
Control EnvironmentThe "tone at the top" — management's attitude toward controls and ethics
Risk AssessmentIdentifying and analyzing risks that could prevent achieving objectives
Control ActivitiesPolicies and procedures that ensure management directives are carried out
Information & CommunicationSystems that capture and share information needed for control duties
MonitoringOngoing assessment of control quality and effectiveness over time

Limitations of Internal Control & Fraud Triangle

Exam Favorite

Internal controls are applied by people, which creates inherent limitations:

Human error: Negligence, fatigue, misjudgment, or confusion can cause mistakes even with good controls in place.

Human fraud: Intentionally defeating controls for personal gain (e.g., management override, collusion).

Fraud Triangle — three conditions present when fraud occurs:

ElementMeaningExample
OpportunityAbility to commit fraudWeak internal controls, lack of separation of duties
PressureMotivation to commit fraudFinancial difficulties, unrealistic performance targets
RationalizationJustification for the act"I'll pay it back," "The company owes me"

Cost-benefit principle: The cost of internal controls must not exceed their benefits. A small store may not need the same controls as a large corporation.

Blockchain technology: A more secure type of accounting ledger that continuously and simultaneously updates and verifies records. Changes cannot be destroyed or hidden, making it harder to commit fraud.

Cash, Cash Equivalents & Liquidity

Key Concept

Cash includes: currency, coins, deposits in bank accounts (checking, savings), customer checks, cashier checks, certified checks, and money orders.

Cash equivalents are short-term, highly liquid investments meeting two criteria:

1. Readily convertible to a known cash amount
2. Close to maturity — market value is not sensitive to interest rate changes (typically purchased within 3 months of maturity)

Examples of cash equivalents: U.S. Treasury bills, money market funds, commercial paper.

NOT cash equivalents: Stocks, accounts receivable, prepaid expenses, or investments maturing beyond 3 months.

Liquidity: A company's ability to pay short-term obligations. Cash and similar assets are called liquid assets because they are readily used to pay current liabilities.

Cash management goals:

1. Plan cash receipts to meet cash payments when due
2. Keep a minimum level of cash necessary to operate

Cash management strategies: Encourage collection of receivables, delay payment of liabilities (without harming credit), keep only necessary assets, plan expenditures, invest excess cash.

Control of Cash Receipts

Key Concept

An effective system for controlling cash should meet three basic guidelines:

1. Handling cash is separated from recordkeeping for cash
2. Cash receipts are promptly deposited in a bank
3. Cash payments are made by check or EFT (not cash)

Over-the-counter receipts: Cash register records each sale electronically. The clerk, clerk's supervisor, and cashier each have separate roles — no single person can divert cash without the difference being revealed.

Cash receipts by mail: At least two people open mail. One lists sender name, amount, and check number. Cashier deposits cash. Recordkeeper records in accounting. A third person reconciles.

Cash Over and Short

Journal Entry

Errors in making change create differences between the cash register record and the actual cash count.

Cash Overage (more cash than register shows):

Register shows $550 in sales, but cash count = $555
Dr. Cash ........................... 555
Cr. Cash Over and Short ...... 5
Cr. Sales .......................... 550

Cash Shortage (less cash than register shows):

Register shows $625 in sales, but cash count = $621
Dr. Cash ........................... 621
Dr. Cash Over and Short ...... 4
Cr. Sales .......................... 625

Year-end reporting: Debit balance (net shortages) → reported as Miscellaneous Expense. Credit balance (net overages) → reported as Miscellaneous Revenue.

Petty Cash Fund

Journal Entry

Used for small payments: postage, courier fees, minor repairs, low-cost supplies. Avoids the hassle of writing checks for tiny amounts.

Three key operations:

1. Establishing the fund:

Dr. Petty Cash .................. 75
Cr. Cash .......................... 75

2. Making payments: Petty cashier disburses cash for approved expenses and collects a signed receipt for each payment. No journal entry is made when individual payments occur.

3. Replenishing the fund — Petty Cash is NOT debited (only debited when establishing or changing fund size):

Dr. Merchandise Inventory .... 15.05 (transportation-in)
Dr. Office Supplies Expense .. 4.75
Dr. Delivery Expense .......... 5.00
Dr. Miscellaneous Expense ... 46.50
Cr. Cash .......................... 71.30
Shortage/Overage: If receipts + remaining cash ≠ fund balance:
• Shortage: Debit Cash Over and Short for the difference
• Overage: Credit Cash Over and Short for the difference

Changing fund size: Increase → Dr. Petty Cash, Cr. Cash. Decrease → Dr. Cash, Cr. Petty Cash.

Bank Reconciliation — Process

Exam Favorite

A bank reconciliation explains why the bank statement balance differs from the company's book balance. Both are adjusted to the same correct (adjusted) balance.

BANK SIDE
Bank statement balanceStarting point
+ Deposits in transitRecorded by company, not yet by bank
− Outstanding checksWritten by company, not yet cleared by bank
± Bank errorsMistakes made by bank
= Adjusted bank balance
BOOK SIDE
Book (company) balanceStarting point
+ Collections by bankNotes collected, interest earned, EFT receipts
− Bank chargesService fees, check printing, NSF checks
± Book errorsMistakes made by company
= Adjusted book balance
Adjusted Bank Balance MUST = Adjusted Book Balance
Critical rule: Only items on the BOOK side require adjusting journal entries. Bank-side items (deposits in transit, outstanding checks) are already recorded in the company's books — the bank just hasn't processed them yet.

Credit memo: Bank increased your account (add to book side)
Debit memo: Bank decreased your account (subtract from book side)

Days' Sales Uncollected

Formula

Measures how quickly a company can convert accounts receivable into cash. Used to assess liquidity.

Days' Sales Uncollected = (Accounts Receivable / Net Sales) × 365

A lower number is preferred — it means faster collection of receivables and stronger liquidity.

Example: Net Sales = $490,000; Accounts Receivable = $31,000
Days' Sales Uncollected = ($31,000 / $490,000) × 365 = 23.1 days

This means it takes approximately 23 days to collect receivables in cash.

Voucher System & Purchase Documents

Key Concept

A voucher system is a set of procedures and approvals designed to control cash disbursements and verify, approve, and record liabilities before payment.

Document flow for a typical purchase:

#DocumentWho Creates ItPurpose
1Purchase RequisitionRequesting departmentInternal request sent TO purchasing dept.
2Purchase OrderPurchasing departmentFormal order sent TO the vendor (supplier)
3InvoiceVendor (supplier)Bill listing items, quantities, prices, and credit terms
4Receiving ReportReceiving departmentDocuments goods received, quantity, and condition
5Invoice ApprovalAccounting departmentChecklist verifying all documents match (also called check authorization)
6VoucherAccounting departmentInternal file accumulating all supporting docs; authorizes recording and payment
Why this matters: Each document is prepared by a different department, creating a system of checks and balances. No single person controls the entire purchasing process. The accounting department doesn't approve payment until all documents agree.

Control of Cash Payments

Key Concept

Most large thefts are from payment of fictitious invoices, making control of cash payments critical.

Keys to controlling cash payments:

1. Require all payments to be made by check (creates a paper trail)
2. Limit check access to authorized persons only
3. Use a cash budget (projected cash receipts and payments) to plan

Gross vs. Net Method of recording purchases:

FeatureGross MethodNet Method
Records invoice atFull (gross) priceDiscounted (net) price
Discount takenCredited to InventoryNo extra entry needed
Discount missedNo entry (discount hidden)Debited to Discounts Lost (visible!)
Management controlWeaker — lost discounts invisibleStronger — lost discounts highlighted
$2,000 invoice, terms 2/10, n/30:

Gross: Record at $2,000. Pay within 10 days → pay $1,960 + credit Inventory $40
Net: Record at $1,960. Pay after discount → pay $2,000 + debit Discounts Lost $40

The net method is preferred because it flags lost discounts as an expense, alerting management to inefficiency.
