Understanding Child Support in Colorado

Child support is designed to ensure that children maintain an appropriate standard of living regardless of which parent they're with. In Colorado, both parents have a legal obligation to support their children financially, and the state has established guidelines to determine fair support amounts.

Whether you're establishing an initial child support order, seeking a modification due to changed circumstances, or facing enforcement issues, having knowledgeable legal representation helps ensure that support calculations are accurate and that your children's needs are met.

At Kovarsky Law, we help parents navigate the complexities of child support to achieve fair outcomes that prioritize children's wellbeing.

How Child Support Is Calculated

Colorado uses an "income shares" model that considers both parents' incomes and the amount of time each parent spends with the children. Key factors include:

Gross Income

Both parents' gross incomes from all sources are considered, including wages, bonuses, commissions, self-employment income, investment income, and certain benefits.

Parenting Time

The number of overnights each parent has with the children affects the calculation. More parenting time typically reduces the support obligation for that parent.

Additional Expenses

Childcare costs, health insurance premiums, and extraordinary medical expenses are factored into the calculation and typically shared proportionally.

Adjustments

Certain deductions may apply, including support obligations for other children, maintenance (alimony) payments, and specific allowable adjustments.

Common Questions

How long does child support last?

In Colorado, child support generally continues until the child turns 19, or 21 if still in high school. Support may end earlier if the child becomes emancipated, marries, or joins the military.

Can child support be modified?

Yes, either parent can request modification if there's been a substantial and continuing change in circumstances. This includes significant income changes, changes in parenting time, or changes in the children's needs.

What if the other parent isn't paying?

Colorado has various enforcement mechanisms including wage garnishment, interception of tax refunds, license suspension, and contempt of court proceedings. We can help you pursue appropriate enforcement actions.

Does joint custody eliminate child support?

Not necessarily. Even with equal parenting time, if there's a significant income disparity between parents, child support may still be ordered to help equalize the children's standard of living in both homes.
