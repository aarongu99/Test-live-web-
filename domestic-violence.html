Protection Orders in Colorado

Colorado offers several types of protection orders to help keep victims safe:

Temporary Protection Order (TPO)

A TPO can be obtained quickly, often the same day you file. It provides immediate protection until a hearing can be held, typically within 14 days. No advance notice to the other party is required.

Permanent Protection Order (PPO)

After a hearing where both parties can present evidence, the court may issue a permanent protection order. Despite the name, these orders can be modified or dismissed under certain circumstances. A PPO can remain in effect indefinitely.

Emergency Protection Order

When courts are closed, law enforcement can request an emergency protection order from a judge. These orders are temporary and must be followed up with a civil protection order.

Common Questions

What qualifies as domestic violence?

Colorado law defines domestic violence broadly to include physical abuse, threats, harassment, stalking, emotional abuse, and controlling behaviors between people in intimate relationships or family members.

Do I need evidence to get a protection order?

While evidence strengthens your case, you can obtain a temporary protection order based on your sworn statement. For a permanent order, presenting evidence such as photos, messages, witness statements, or police reports can be helpful.

What happens if a protection order is violated?

Violating a protection order is a criminal offense in Colorado. Violators can be arrested and face criminal charges, fines, and jail time. Document any violations and report them to law enforcement.

How does domestic violence affect custody?

Courts take domestic violence very seriously when determining custody. Evidence of abuse can result in restrictions on parenting time, requirements for supervised visitation, and other protective measures for children.
