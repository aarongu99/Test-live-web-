Understanding Child Custody in Colorado

When it comes to your children, nothing matters more than ensuring their wellbeing and maintaining your relationship with them. Colorado law focuses on arrangements that serve the "best interests of the child," and understanding how custody works is essential for any parent facing separation or divorce.

In Colorado, we use the terms "parental responsibilities" and "parenting time" rather than "custody" and "visitation." This reflects the modern understanding that both parents typically play important roles in their children's lives, even after separation.

At Kovarsky Law, we help parents develop parenting plans that work for their families, advocate for fair parenting time arrangements, and protect parental rights when disputes arise.

Key Components of Parental Responsibilities

Decision-Making Responsibility

This covers major decisions about your child's education, healthcare, religious upbringing, and extracurricular activities. Parents may share decision-making jointly, or one parent may have sole authority in specific areas.

Parenting Time

This determines when your child is with each parent. Schedules can range from equal time arrangements to primary residence with one parent and regular visitation with the other, depending on what works best for your family.

Parenting Plan

Colorado requires a detailed parenting plan that outlines decision-making authority, parenting time schedules, holiday arrangements, communication protocols, and procedures for resolving future disputes.

How Courts Determine Best Interests

Colorado courts consider numerous factors when determining custody arrangements, including:

  • The wishes of the parents and, if appropriate, the child
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community
  • The mental and physical health of all parties
  • Each parent's ability to encourage the child's relationship with the other parent
  • The past involvement of each parent in the child's life
  • The proximity of the parents' homes
  • Each parent's ability to place the child's needs above their own

Common Questions

At what age can a child choose which parent to live with?

Colorado has no specific age at which a child can choose. Courts may consider a child's preference as one factor, giving it more weight as the child matures, but the child's wishes are never the sole determining factor.

Is joint custody the default in Colorado?

Colorado doesn't have a presumption of joint custody, but courts generally favor arrangements that allow children to have meaningful relationships with both parents, unless there are concerns about safety or wellbeing.

Can I modify our custody arrangement?

Modifications are possible when there's been a substantial and continuing change in circumstances that affects the child's best interests. Minor scheduling adjustments may be made by agreement, while significant changes typically require court approval.

What if we can't agree on custody?

Colorado requires mediation before contested custody cases go to court. If mediation doesn't resolve all issues, the court will make decisions based on the evidence presented and the child's best interests.
